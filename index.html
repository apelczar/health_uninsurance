Health (Un)Insurance in the US

by Ali Pelczar

In 2018, about 90% of all Americans under 65 had some form of health insurance. The remainder, more than 28 million people, were uninsured. Dive deeper into how that 10% is distributed across several dimensions, and how that number has changed over time.

All states have seen a decrease in previous years in the percent of residents uninsured. This primarily occurred in 2014 and 2015 when much of the Affordable Care Act came into effect.

New Mexico saw the greatest absolute decrease during this time. It more than halved its uninsured rate, from 24% in 2008 to 11% in 2018.

Massachusetts implemented major health care reform in 2006 and entered this period with an uninsured rate much lower than any other state. It has changed little since 2008.

Meanwhile, Texas both started and ended this period with the highest uninsured rate in the country.

Under the Affordable Care Act, states had the option to expand eligibility for Medicaid. By June 2019, these 34 states had chosen to do so.

The states that haven't expanded Medicaid are generally being left behind in terms of the uninsured rate.
